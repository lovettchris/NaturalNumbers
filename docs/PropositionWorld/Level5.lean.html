Level 5 : P ‚Üí (Q ‚Üí P).

In this level, our goal is to construct an implication, like in level 2.

‚ä¢ P ‚Üí (Q ‚Üí P)

So P and Q are propositions, and our goal is to prove that P ‚üπ(Q ‚üπ P). We don't know whether P, Q are true or false, so initially this seems like a bit of a tall order. But let's give it a go.

Our goal is P ‚Üí X for some true/false statement X, and if our goal is to construct an implication then we almost always want to use the intro tactic from level 2, Lean's version of "assume P", or more precisely "assume p is a proof of P". So let's start with

intro p

and we then find ourselves in this tactic state:

P Q : Prop,
p : P
‚ä¢ Q ‚Üí P

We now have a proof p of P and we are supposed to be constructing a proof of Q ‚üπ P. So let's assume that Q is true and try and prove that P is true. We assume Q like this:

intro q

and now we have to prove P, but have a proof handy:

exact p

A mathematician would treat the proposition P ‚üπ(Q ‚üπ P) as the same as the proposition P ‚àß Q ‚üπ P, because to give a proof of either of these is just to give a method which takes a proof of P and a proof of Q, and returns a proof of P. Did you notice?

I wrote P ‚Üí (Q ‚Üí P) but Lean just writes P ‚Üí Q ‚Üí P. This is because computer scientists adopt the convention that ‚Üí is right associative, which is a fancy way of saying "when we write P ‚Üí Q ‚Üí R, we mean P ‚Üí (Q ‚Üí R). Mathematicians would never dream of writing something as ambiguous as P ‚üπ Q ‚üπ R (they are not really interested in proving abstract propositions, they would rather work with concrete ones such as Fermat's Last Theorem), so they do not have a convention for where the brackets go. It's important to remember Lean's convention though, or else you will get confused. If your goal is P ‚Üí Q ‚Üí R then you need to know whether intro h will create h : P or h : P ‚Üí Q. Make sure you understand which one.
